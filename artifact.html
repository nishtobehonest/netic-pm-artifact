The enterprise motion changes the benchmark. Netic's Series B funds the push into multi-year, multi-product enterprise relationships. Enterprise customers don't renew on "jobs booked." 02 — Root Cause

The pipeline has a black hole.
It lives between dispatch and the Cultivate Engine.

Three products. One missing link. The Netic Brain's intelligence stops at the dispatcher — it never reaches the technician, and field outcomes never feed back to improve targeting.

The amber nodes are where the artifact focuses — both are inferred from the absence of any technician-side features in public product descriptions. See Tab 6.

Competitive context worth naming: ServiceTitan (dominant FSM in HVAC) has a technician mobile app with customer history and job notes. The differentiation Netic can credibly claim is AI-native reasoning — not static records a tech scrolls through, but real-time synthesis of equipment age, weather signals, and conversion history surfaced at the right moment. That's the layer ServiceTitan can't retrofit without rearchitecting their data model.
Three bottlenecks at the gap
Bottleneck 01

Technician arrives blind

No AI briefing with customer history, equipment age, or upsell flags. Decisions are gut-based and inconsistent across technicians — the value of the Netic Brain never reaches the person actually at the job site.

Bottleneck 02

Upsell is human and variable

Whether a customer hears about a membership or filter upgrade depends on which technician shows up, their energy level, and time pressure on the next job. No structure, no prompting, no consistency across the fleet.

Bottleneck 03

Post-job outcome is unstructured

Maybe a CRM note gets written. Often nothing does. The Cultivate Engine cannot learn which customers convert to memberships, which upsell prompts work for which segments, or which technicians outperform.

The structural problem
The Cultivate Engine is running open-loop. It sends signals — outbound demand via weather triggers and customer data — but receives no signal back. It doesn't know which outbound campaigns converted, which upsell prompts landed, or which customers became members. Without the feedback loop, it can't improve. It's optimizing on assumptions, not outcomes.
If I were on this team, I'd ask: What percentage of Cultivate Engine outbound campaigns do we know converted — with enough specificity to retrain the targeting model? My hypothesis is that number is low, and the absence of a structured feedback channel is why.

03 — The Framework

Not a rebuild. An extension.
Three phases to close the loop.

The booking stack works — don't touch it. This is an addition: push structured intelligence to the technician, and pull structured feedback back from the field. Minimal behavior change required at each phase.

Phased rollout
Phase Timeframe What it does Behavior change required
Phase 1
Pre-job briefing card		 8–12 weeks AI summary pushed to technician's phone 10 min before arrival. Customer history, equipment age, open recommendations. Read-only. Timeline assumes FSM integration complexity — ServiceTitan/Jobber API reliability varies. Zero — technician just reads it
Phase 2
On-job upsell prompts		 +8–10 weeks after P1 After job marked "in progress," AI surfaces 1–2 specific nudges based on customer profile. Tech taps accept or decline. Minimal — one tap
Phase 3
Feedback loop to Cultivate Engine		 Ongoing Accept/decline + outcome data flows back automatically. Engine learns which prompts convert for which segments and retrains targeting model. None — fully automated

Phasing prioritizes zero disruption to the existing booking flow. Each phase ships independently and is independently measurable.

Before and after
Dimension Before After
Technician intel None — arrives blind to customer history and equipment state AI briefing card 10 min pre-arrival with customer history, equipment age, open recommendations
Upsell consistency Gut-based; varies by technician, energy level, time pressure AI-prompted, structured — same quality of nudge regardless of which technician shows up
Post-job data Unstructured CRM note or absent entirely Structured accept/decline + conversion outcome captured per job
Cultivate Engine learning Static targeting model — no signal from the field Self-improving from field outcomes — closed-loop, retrained continuously
The framing I'd bring to this internally: "We're not changing the booking product. We're extending the intelligence layer one hop further — from the dispatcher to the technician — and creating the return channel the Cultivate Engine is missing." The constraint is technician adoption. That's why Phase 1 requires zero behavior change — read-only is the wedge.
The technician incentive problem — and why Phase 1 is designed around it. HVAC technicians are typically paid hourly or flat-rate. Their incentive is to finish jobs fast and move to the next one — upsells add time, and they don't always see the upside. A briefing card framed as "more tasks" will get ignored. Phase 1 is read-only deliberately: the value proposition to the tech is less cognitive load ("here's everything you need before you walk in") not more. Phase 2's single-tap prompt is designed around the same constraint — one decision, no typing, under 10 seconds. If commission structures can be tied to upsell outcomes, that's a separate lever worth exploring with operator partners in the pilot.
Framework designed around the constraint of minimal technician behavior change — the most common failure mode in field service software rollouts is tech-side friction that kills adoption before the product can prove value.
04 — Metrics

How I'd know if the hypothesis is right.
And exactly how I'd know if it isn't.

Three primary metrics, three weekly leading indicators, and a failure mode map. The failure modes matter as much as the success metrics — each one points to a specific upstream cause, not just "it didn't work."

Primary north star metrics
Primary North Star

Average job value ($)

Baseline vs. 30/60/90 day post-rollout. If upsell prompts and briefing cards are working, this moves. If it doesn't, the hypothesis is wrong or the intervention point is wrong.

Leading Indicator

Upsell attach rate (%)

% of jobs with at least one accepted upsell. Moves faster than avg job value — confirms whether technicians are converting prompted opportunities, not just whether upsells are being offered.

Long-term Signal

Membership conversion rate (%)

% of jobs converting to a recurring plan. The highest-value outcome — more important than single upsells. This is the metric a customer QBR should lead with after 90 days.

Engine Health

Cultivate Engine precision

Did AI-targeted outreach convert? Measured before (open-loop baseline) vs. after (closed-loop with field feedback). This is the proof that closing the loop improves the targeting model.

Weekly leading indicators (health checks)
Briefing card view rate — are technicians opening it before arriving? Target: >60%
Prompt response rate — accept or decline tapped on upsell nudges Target: >40% response
Feedback data completeness — % of jobs with structured post-job capture Target: >80%
Failure mode map — because "it didn't work" isn't diagnostic
If this happens It means Response
Briefing view rate <40% UX friction — the card isn't useful enough to open Simplify the card format; push via SMS instead of in-app; reduce information density
Prompt accept rate <5% Prompts are irrelevant to the job context Audit the targeting model — wrong segment, wrong timing, or wrong upsell type being surfaced
Avg job value flat despite prompts Upsell pricing is the problem, not awareness Not a PM problem — pricing or product issue upstream. Escalate to product/pricing team
Tech NPS drops Briefing/prompts feel intrusive or add pressure Reduce prompt frequency; add opt-out for technicians; reframe tools as helpful, not mandatory
If I were on this team, I'd run this as a 60-day pilot with a single customer and 5–10 technicians. Primary metric: avg job value at day 60 vs. baseline. Secondary: briefing view rate by day 7. If view rate doesn't hit 40% in week one, the UX is wrong — don't wait 60 days to find out.
Metric targets are hypotheses, not guarantees — I'd calibrate them against Netic's existing baseline data in the first two weeks. The framework is the structure; the numbers need ground truth from the product team.
